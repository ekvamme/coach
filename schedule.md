## Goals

**Near-term (4–8 weeks, May–June 2026)**
- Increase climbing from 1× to 2×/week without flaring shoulder symptoms (drift-free, by the pain-monitoring model)
- Establish a known, stable post-workout symptom baseline — log resolution time week over week
- Establish a consistent long aerobic day on Saturday (hiking + running base)
- Preserve every PT staple — none get dropped
- Bottom-up TGU at 20 lb, clean reps both sides
- Hip mobility baseline locked in: couch stretch, 90/90, pancake — daily, no skips

**Far-term (3–12 months, through Dec 2026)**
- **Onsight or redpoint Yosemite 5.10b–5.10c trad in fall 2026** (mid-September → late October target window)
- **Long-term aspiration: zero post-workout shoulder pain** as a marker the tendon has fully remodeled. Operationally, manage by the pain-monitoring model — mild burning that resolves is OK; drift is not.
- Climb 2–3×/week sustainably
- Tolerate full-night left-side sleeping with no morning discomfort
- Aerobic fitness floor: long trail runs with your friend that don't leave you whooped, 25–30 mi 2-day thru hikes without specific prep
- CIM (Dec 6 2026) is optional. Run it only if fall fitness lines up.
- Maintain hip flexibility year-round: pancake / couch-stretch / 90/90 ROM should be the same or better in December than today
- Reintroduce overhead pressing pain-free (likely around month 4–6, progressed cautiously)
- Bottom-up TGU at 25 lb

## Two-phase plan (now → end of 2026)

Climbing is the priority year-round. Running and hiking are paced to support it, not compete with it. So the year only has two phases:

| Phase | Window | Runs/wk | Long run / hike | Climbing | Focus |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **1 — Base + shoulder** | May → mid-July (10–12 wk) | 2 | 8–10 mi run *or* 2–3 hr hike | 1 → 2×/wk | Shoulder rehab; climbing volume ramp; aerobic base; lock in mobility habits |
| **2 — Maintain** | Mid-July → end of 2026 | 2–3 | 10–12 mi run *or* 3–4 hr hike, occasional 15 mi | 2–3×/wk | Maintain everything; bias to climbing during Valley windows (May–June, Sep) |

That's it. There's no peak. Long runs cap around 12–15 mi at comfortable conversational pace — enough for fun outings with your running partner, enough that a 25–30 mi 2-day thru hike is a non-event, and enough that *if* you decide to run CIM in December you can show up and survive (not race it). If you decide in October you do want to push CIM, you can add 2–3 longer road long-runs in Nov as soft prep — but that's an opt-in, not a default.

Why this works:
- Climbing recovery isn't competing with marathon volume in October–November (prime Valley shoulder season)
- Aerobic fitness drift takes much longer than people think — once you have base, *maintaining* it is cheap. 2 runs/wk + a long Saturday holds the fitness floor.
- Long runs grow only if and when you want them to. Standard rule still applies: **+10%/week max, cutback every 4th week**, but you almost never need to push the ceiling.

## Daily mobility (the most important non-negotiable)

Running miles without daily mobility work is exactly how runners turn into bricks. This protects the lower extremity flexibility you flagged as a goal — and it's also climbing performance work.

**Every morning, 5–7 min** (do this *before* coffee — it removes the friction):
- Cat-cow + thread the needle, 8 each
- World's greatest stretch, 5 each side
- 90/90 hip switches, 8 each side
- Couch stretch, 60 s each side (this is the single highest-ROI movement for runners)

**After every run (10 min, non-negotiable on long-run day)**:
- Standing calf stretch, both knee-straight and knee-bent (gastroc + soleus), 60 s each
- Active hamstring (hand on wall, leg-up scoop), 10 each
- Couch stretch again, 90 s each side
- Pigeon, 60 s each side
- Foam roller: glutes, IT band, quads, calves — 90 s per area

**Two longer mobility blocks per week** (15–20 min, e.g., Friday and Sunday morning):
- Pancake stretch (active + passive)
- Frog stretch
- Deep squat hold, 2 min
- Hip CARs (controlled articular rotations), 5 each direction each side
- Ankle dorsiflexion mobilization (knee-to-wall), 10 each side

Treat these like sets in a strength program — log them, don't negotiate them. They protect both your running tolerance and your climbing range.

## Stop-light system (read this once, refer to it weekly)

Operating standard: **standard pain-monitoring model.** Zero post-workout pain is the long-term aspiration — what you'll see naturally as the tendon finishes remodeling — but day-to-day, mild post-load burning that resolves is acceptable. The killer is *drift*, not absolute level.

- **Green** — pain-free *during* exercise; any post-workout burning fades within ~24 hours; sleep stable or improving. Continue or progress.
- **Yellow** — burning lingers more than 24 hours, sleep regresses for one night, *or* burning intensity is creeping up week over week. Hold loads flat, look for the trigger (most likely home tension board, then climbing intensity, then the top end of bottom-up TGU). If it persists 2 weeks, dial the suspected trigger back 10–20%.
- **Red** — pain *during* exercise, sharp pain anywhere, sleep regresses multiple nights, or symptoms progressively worse for 2+ weeks. Cut climbing volume in half, drop board/fingerboard work, message your PT.

**Drift anchor (confirmed 2026-04-28):** post-workout pain runs ~3/10 in the *anterior + superior* left shoulder. That number is the baseline to compare against, not the threshold to react to. A single 4/10 day means nothing; sustained 4+/10 across 2+ sessions, *or* baseline pattern shifting to a new location, is real drift.

What to track weekly so you can spot drift: did this week's post-workout burning *resolve faster, the same, or slower* than last week's? Trend matters more than any single session.

## Saturday alternates

**Week A — Hiking focus**
- Long hilly hike, 2–4 hours, conversational pace
- Start with a light day pack; progress to weighted pack (5 → 15 → 25 lb) as the season approaches your thru-hike target

**Week B — Climbing focus**
- Home tension board session, **30–45 min only**, **vertical to slight overhang only** (don't crank the wall to 30° yet — the cuff isn't ready for max-overhang volume on top of a quarry day)
- Followed by a 60–90 min easy bike or run

Alternate weekly until you're 4 weeks symptom-free at this volume — then make Week B the default and the schedule becomes 2× climbing / week.

- Warm-up on the bike ride over (zone 2 — the commute IS your warm-up)

## What's intentionally NOT in here yet

These come back in once the shoulder is more robust. Do not add them on your own:

- **Overhead pressing** (any variant) — re-introduce around weeks 8–12 starting with **landmine press** (the ROM is the safest re-entry), then half-kneeling DB press, then standing. Only after 4+ weeks of green status.
- **Fingerboard work** — hold off. Add only after 4 weeks of symptom-free 2×/week climbing. When you do add it, start with submaximal **max hangs** (10s @ ~70% effort), 1 short session/week, separated from climbing days by 48 hr.
- **Full 30° tension board sessions** — the wall is great, but max-overhang volume is high cuff load. Vertical/slight overhang only for now.
- **Freestyle/backstroke swimming** — skip indefinitely; not worth it given the symptomatic zone.
- **Dips, behind-the-neck anything, upright rows, kipping** — not in this program at all.

## Progression rules

1. **Hold a load/volume for 2 weeks before adding** to it. The cuff remodels on a slow timeline.
2. **Climbing volume is the master variable.** When you increase it, hold strength volume flat. Don't progress both at once.
3. **Sleep tolerance on the left side is your best long-term gauge.** Trending better = the program is working. Trending worse = something is too much; the most likely culprit is climbing volume or board angle.
4. **Bike commute counts.** Don't double-cardio on Mon (Quarry day) — the ride is enough.
5. **PT staples are non-negotiable.** They go in every week regardless of how the rest of the schedule shifts.

## Climbing for Yosemite 5.10b/c — fall 2026 timeline

Given where you are — sessioning 10b at Donner, redpointing 11a sport, leading classic Yosemite 5.9 trad, alpine days like East Buttress of Middle Cath under your belt — the goal is the next stretch grade. The work is **converting sessioning 10b → onsighting/redpointing 10b/c on trad** by **mid-September to late October 2026**.

### Climbing build calendar (5 months from now)

| Window | Climbs/wk | Focus |
|---|---|---|
| **May (now) — Establish baseline** (3–4 wk) | 1, then 1+light | Quarry day at moderate intensity. Track post-workout burning resolution time as your baseline. Add a light home board session (vertical to slight overhang only) at the end of May if green for 2 weeks. |
| **June — Lock in 2× / week** | 2 | Two real climbing sessions: Quarry + light home board. Hold for 2+ weeks of green status before stepping up intensity. Yosemite/Donner weekend trips when possible — these *are* climbing sessions and replace the home board that week. |
| **July — Add ARC and fingerboard** | 2 | One climbing session/week becomes ARC-style (continuous easy, 20–40 min). The other stays a real climb. Submaximal fingerboard work enters if 4 weeks of green — density / submax max hangs only. |
| **August — Build pyramid** | 2–3 | Trad pyramid: lots of 5.9 ticks, multiple attempts on chosen 10a/10b trad projects at Donner. If shoulder is consistently green, third climbing session/week. |
| **September → late October — Send window** | 2–3 | Yosemite trips. Project-attempt phase. By now you should have 10a trad onsight tight and 10b sessioning at Donner clean. Start tagging Yosemite 10a/b → 10c.  |
| **November–December** — climbing maintains, optional CIM tail | 2 | After fall send window, drop intensity. Easy climbing keeps the engine warm without shoulder load. |

**The first month is the most important.** Establish your baseline: what's the burning's pattern at your current load? When does it resolve? Track this, week over week, so you have a real reference for what "drift" looks like later. The goal isn't to start from zero pain — it's to start with a *known, stable* response so you can spot when something's getting worse.

### What actually moves the needle (in order)

1. **Pitch volume on real granite at the right grade.** The Quarry rope solo + Donner Pass weekends are your bread and butter. Trad pyramid building: lots of 5.9 to lock in headpoint, multiple attempts on chosen 10a/10b trad projects, then start ticking 10c. Donner is the right intermediate proving ground — granite cracks of similar character to the Valley.
2. **Crack hand durability.** You know how this works; just don't skip the boring part. Tape early on long crack days; let the skin and joints adapt over months, not weeks.
3. **Pitch-length endurance — ARC training.** Worth adding once shoulder tolerates 2 climbs/week. 20–40 min continuous climbing on the home board at vertical to slight overhang, easy-to-moderate moves, never pumped. One session/week.
4. **Submaximal fingerboard work** — added only after 4 weeks of symptom-free 2×/week climbing. Density hangs and submaximal max hangs, not max-effort crimping. The cuff and pulleys both gate this.
5. **30° tension board** — keep using it for general climbing fitness, low-angle and moderate intensity. It's a great tool, just not the lever for Yosemite trad. Don't crank the wall steep and chase max-effort sessions while shoulder tendinosis is healing.

### Valley windows in 2026

- **May → mid-June** — *right now*. Best window before summer heat. Use it for shakedown trips: Donner cracks at 10a/b, easier Yosemite multi-pitch to scrub the rust off, get a feel for the shoulder under real route conditions.
- **Mid-September → late October** — **the send window.** This is where the fall 2026 timeline lands. Project-attempt mode.
- **November–December** — cool/cold, fewer crowds. Maintenance climbing.

If 10b/c isn't ticked by late October, **spring 2027** is the next clean window — that's not a failure, that's the calendar.

All trails by default. If you decide in October to run CIM, throw in 2–3 longer road long-runs in November to wake up the road-pounding mechanics — that's enough prep to survive the race, not race it. Otherwise, never any need to leave the trails.
